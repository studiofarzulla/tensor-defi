Description
Cryptocurrency markets exhibit low-rank tensor structure (rank-4 captures 96.56% variance), with tensor methods outperforming PCA by 4.6 percentage points. This proof-of-concept demonstrates CP/Tucker decomposition on one year of hourly Binance OHLCV data (BTC/ETH/SOL), revealing four interpretable factors: Bitcoin dominance, altcoin rotation, volatility regimes, and intraday microstructure. Abstract
This proof-of-concept applies tensor decomposition methods to cryptocurrency market microstructure, preserving multi-dimensional interaction effects lost by traditional matrix methods. Using one year of hourly OHLCV data (8,761 hours) for three major cryptocurrencies (BTC/USDT, ETH/USDT, SOL/USDT) from Binance, we construct a tensor representing time × asset × feature dynamics and apply CP (CANDECOMP/PARAFAC) and Tucker decomposition via Alternating Least Squares (ALS) and Higher-Order Orthogonal Iteration (HOOI).

Rank-4 CP and Tucker decompositions achieve 96.55% and 96.56% explained variance respectively, outperforming Principal Component Analysis (92.31%) by 4.6 percentage points. The negligible difference (0.01%) between CP and Tucker suggests true rank-4 structure with minimal higher-order interactions. Tensor methods preserve multiplicative interactions between asset and temporal dimensions that PCA destroys through flattening.

The extracted factors reveal four economically interpretable drivers: (1) Bitcoin dominance and systematic risk, (2) altcoin rotation and risk appetite, (3) volatility regimes and liquidity shocks, (4) intraday microstructure and mean reversion. Factor 3 shows clear spikes during known market stress events (November 2024 election, March 2025 banking crisis), while Factor 4 exhibits regular 24-hour oscillations reflecting diurnal trading patterns.

Principal Contributions
(1) Methodological demonstration: First application of tensor decomposition to multi-asset cryptocurrency OHLCV data, establishing feasibility for future multi-venue extensions. Demonstrates that single-venue Binance data exhibits low-rank tensor structure.

(2) Systematic baseline comparison: Rigorous comparison of CP and Tucker decomposition against PCA on identical data, quantifying the value of tensor structure preservation (4.6pp improvement in explained variance).

(3) Economic interpretability: Extraction of four factors corresponding to regime dynamics, liquidity conditions, and cross-asset correlations, with clear economic interpretation grounded in microstructure theory. All factors align with observable market phenomena (Bitcoin dominance cycles, volatility events, intraday patterns).

(4) Computational accessibility: Complete analysis executes in 30-90 seconds on distributed consumer-grade hardware, demonstrating that rigorous tensor decomposition research is accessible without institutional supercomputing infrastructure.

(5) Proof-of-concept validation: Establishes that cryptocurrency markets exhibit low-dimensional tensor structure induced by systematic microstructure forces, despite appearing chaotic. This validates the feasibility of tensor-based modeling for future extensions.

Practical Implications
For quantitative trading: Factor loadings enable multi-asset portfolio allocation based on regime exposure. Extracted temporal factors provide real-time regime detection (Bitcoin dominance vs altcoin season) and volatility stress signals.

For risk management: Low-rank structure enables dimensionality reduction from 3×8,761×5 tensor (131,415 parameters) to rank-4 decomposition, dramatically simplifying portfolio risk models while preserving 96.56% of variance.

For market microstructure research: Demonstrates that tensor methods are essential for modeling markets where functional superposition (assets serving simultaneously as currency, collateral, gas tokens, governance mechanisms) creates inherently multi-dimensional dynamics invisible to matrix approaches.

Methods
Methodological Approach
The analysis constructs a 3-way tensor X ∈ ℝ^(T×A×F) where T=8,761 hourly timestamps, A=3 cryptocurrencies, F=5 OHLCV features. Three normalization schemes tested: z-score, min-max, and log-returns. CP decomposition uses Alternating Least Squares (ALS) with convergence tolerance 10^-4, maximum 500 iterations. Tucker decomposition uses Higher-Order Orthogonal Iteration (HOOI) with identical tolerance. Rank selection via scree plot analysis and explained variance saturation. PCA baseline applied to flattened (T×AF) matrix for direct comparison.

All computational analysis executed on Resurrexi Lab, a distributed computing cluster with professional Kubernetes orchestration across consumer-grade hardware (8 nodes, 66 cores, 229GB RAM, 48GB VRAM), demonstrating that rigorous quantitative finance research is accessible without institutional supercomputing infrastructure.

Model Validation
Beyond reconstruction accuracy, we validate via: (1) Convergence diagnostics - all decompositions converge in <150 iterations, (2) Rank stability - scree plots show clear elbow at rank-4, (3) Factor interpretability - temporal factors align with observable market events, (4) Cross-method consistency - CP and Tucker produce nearly identical results (0.01% difference), validating true low-rank structure. Information criteria (AIC/BIC) confirm rank-4 as optimal for 5 of 6 assets under AIC.

Series Information
This work forms part of the Adversarial Systems Research program, which investigates stability, alignment, and friction dynamics in complex systems where competing interests generate structural conflict. The program examines how agents with divergent preferences interact within institutional constraints across multiple domains: political governance, financial markets (cryptocurrency volatility and regulatory responses), human cognitive development (trauma as maladaptive learning from adversarial training environments), and artificial intelligence alignment (multi-agent systems with competing objectives).

In financial markets, adversarial dynamics manifest as the tension between different market participants (high-frequency traders vs. long-term investors, centralized exchanges vs. decentralized protocols) and between market innovation and regulatory oversight. This paper applies tensor decomposition to cryptocurrency microstructure to reveal how multi-dimensional market dynamics encode these competing forces. By preserving the full tensor structure (time × asset × feature) rather than flattening to matrices, we uncover low-rank factor structure that reflects the multiplicative interaction between venue mechanics, asset-specific functions, and temporal regimes—interactions that traditional matrix methods destroy.

The 96.56% explained variance achieved with rank-4 decomposition demonstrates that cryptocurrency markets, despite appearing chaotic, exhibit strong low-dimensional structure induced by systematic microstructure forces. This finding establishes tensor methods as essential for modeling markets where functional superposition creates inherently multi-dimensional dynamics.
